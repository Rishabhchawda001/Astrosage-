FOREWORD

\ :
The Vrddhayavanajdtaka shortly known as Yavanajdtaka of Minaraja is the first work on Hindu
astrology appearing in the Gaeckwad’s Oriental Series.

Written in Western India probably in the 4th cent. A.D. the Yavanajdtaka is one of the most impor-
tant contributions of that part of India in the field of astrology. Its publication for the first time will, it is
hoped, throw new light on some of the vexed problems of astrology, such as the cultural and academic
relations of Yavanas ( Greeks ) with our country, their contribution in the field of Indian astrology, and
the mutual influence of Greek and Indian scholars in this branch of study.

Varahamihira refers to one Yavanacérya. His commentator Bhat{a Utpala refers to Yavanesvara
Sphujidhvaja, the author of the Yavanajdtaka ( written in 269 A.D.). This Sphujidhvaja refers to the views
of the Yavanas with the words: “ Yavandh Ocuh’”. This indicates that there was a band of Yavana
astrologers who enriched this field even before Sphujidhvaja.

By as carly as the time of Bhatta Utpala (10th cent. A.D.) their works seem to have been lost.
Fortunately, two works on Jataka by the Yavanas ( or Greeks ) have come down to us: (1) Yavanajdtaka
by Sphujidhvaja and (2) the Yavanajdtaka of Minardja which is als) known as Vrddhayavanajdtaka or
Minardjajataka, The author in the very beginning of his work (1.2) proclaims that he is merely condensing
to 8000 verses only, from the original consisting of one lakh verses, the work of an ancient sage who taught
it to Maya, .

The present work critically and carefully edited by Dr. David Pingree of Brown University ( U.S.A. )
with the help of as many as 16 MSS ( rejecting some half a dozen ones ) should prove an important contri-
bution in the field of astrology inasmuch as it will open a new vista in the field of cultural relations between
ancient Greece and India and the mutual borrowings of the scholars of both these ancient countries. Dr.
Pingree’s third volume consisting of his learned introduction throws valuable light on these cultural aspects.
T hope, Dr. Pingree’s work, would be welcomed by students of Indian astrology and scholars working in the
field of ancient Indian cultural history.

The printing of the present work by the photo-offset printing process of the original manuscript of
the author is not satisfactory in some places. The Ex-Director of the Institute was, however, compelled to
undertake such a printing due to certain unavoidable difficulties. The consequent inconvenience to the
reader is regretted,

J thank Dr. Pingree for accepting our invitation to undertake the task of preparing a critical edition
of this valuable work.

T also thank the University Grants Commission and the Government of Gujarat for jointly giving
financial help towards the publication of this volume.

Oriental Institute, AN. JANL

INTRODUCTION

This, the first of three projected volumes, contains adhydyas 1-39 of the Vrddhayavanajataka composed
by Minaraja in western India, probably in the fourth century 4.D. The second volume will contain the
remainder of the text ( adhydyas 40-71 ) and an index of verses, while the third will contain an introduction
discussing the manuscript tradition and influence of the Vrddhayavanajdtaka and its relations to the
Yavanajdtaka of Sphujidhvaja, written in 269 A.D., and to the lost work of Satya; some supplementary
texts (¢.g., the Strijdtaka and the Yavanasamhitd ) will conclude volume Ill.

As the manuscripts will be fully described in vol, III and their interrelationships clarified, it is

In the edition some examples of faulty grammar and prosody have been allowed to stand as Minaraja’s
ability to compose flawless verses is much to be doubted. Where, however, the manuscripts offer a text
bereft of sense, the offending passage is marked by a dagger. It is clear that the manuscripts are all derived
from one original as they all partake in certain lacunae; but, while the scribes of E, G, and R had access to
an excellent manuscript and often alone preserve the right reading, the scribes of L, Q, and W are often
