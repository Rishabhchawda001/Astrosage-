Foreword

Indian Classical Music is a rich legacy that has come down to us
through ages orally. Musical art and aesthetics and theories are
of equal importance. Both being handed down the ages orally,
Guru has an extraordinarily important role in both the aspects of
music. Receiving direct training from a Guru over years shapes a
musician, and that is our tradition. Intense practice (Riaz) is of
paramount importance. An ardent learner starts his first Riaz
before a Guru, and keeps up the practice singing till end of his
musical career. Whatever the innate talent, a learner can be an
effective musician only through devoted Riaz. Eshita
Chakraborty’s tome “The Importance of Riaz in Indian
Classical Music” is a useful attempt to show the primacy of Riaz
in Classical Music. She has made an elaborate analysis of practice
music (Riaz) as it has come down the ages. The learners of all
times shall immensely benefit from this work — trail-blazer to the
highway of Classical Music. A gifted research work indeed.

About seven swaras — Sargam

At some stage of the development of Indian music, the names —
shadja, rishav, gandhar, madhyam, pancham, dhaivat & nishad,
were given to its seven basic notes to identify them. The abridged
version of these names -sa, re, ga, ma, pa dha & ni — is called
“Sargam”. For instance —

Sa is Shadja

Re is Rishav

Ga is Gandhar

Ma is Madhyam

Pa is Pancham

Dha is Dhaivat

Ni is Nishad

The word ‘Sargam’ itself is based on the first four abriged

note names sa, re, ga & ma. It is totally impractical to pronounce
the full name of the note while learning, teaching or practising
music. As single syllable sa, re ga, ma, pa, dha, ni — Sargam is
much easier to sing than the full note names — Shadja, Rishav,
Gandhar, Madhyam etc. Sargam also makes it much more practical
to do note exercises & intonation exercises. As symbols of
notation, when sargam syllable are sung, the help to
simultaneously produce & identify a note. For example, the
syllable ma is used to sing the pitch ma as well as to identify that
pitch as being ma. The importance of this aspect of sargam is that

it makes the activity of singing more conscious. Even while
thinking of composing music, sargam or note syllables are much
easier to use & more economical than full note-names.

Another advantage of having abridged note names is that in
their monosyllabic form, they help in the perception of & play
with rhythm, Sargam can help in gaining familiarity with the
tempo & rhythmic structure of tala by composing phrases in
such a way that the various rhythmic divisions of the tala become
more obvious. For example, in teentaal, each beat can be sung
with one, two, three or four note names depending on the tempo.
The four divisions of teentaal, i.e. the three talis & one khali, can
be accented by stressing on the sargam syllable that falls on the
first beat of each division. It would be impossible to practice to
be such rhythm oriented exercises if the full note-names like
Shadja, Rishav, Gandhar etc were to be used because the latter
are made up of several syllables like sha-da-ja, ri-sha-va etc. These
kind of exercises also help in learning how to compose extempore
note patterns in various tempos that would automatically fit into
the tala structure. Even while or analysing the structure of music
using full note names would be very difficult & cumbersome also.
Thus although the initial & primary purpose of naming the seven
basic notes must have been identification of those notes, later on
these note-names had to be abridged for the very practical reasons-
the single syllables can perform many functions much more
efficiently. Sargam therefore becomes essential for practising,
leaning, teaching, perceiving, composing, analysing, interpreting
& memorising music.

In Indian Music,

> slow & fast musical phrases that are composed through
vowels are called alaps & taans respectively.

> Phrases composed through words are called —bolalap, bolbant
& boltaan. Phrases composed through the short form of note
names or note syllables sa, re, ga, ma etc. are called Sargam.

From the syllabic nature of bolalap bolbant & boltaan, Sargam
is distinct & unique. The Sargam syllables must coincide with
the note position that they identify i.e. while using the syllable
ni, the note ni must be sung or while using the syllable pa the
note pa must be sung. But the syllables in bolalap & boltaan are
not assigned any particular pitch or note position. The most
important thing to be noted is that Sargam like bolalap, bolbant
& boltaan uses syllables in constructing musical phrases but unlike
the former, sargam syllables have no literary meaning. Instead,
they possess musical meaning. This Sargam is not restricted to
any particular expression, movement or tempo nor is it obstructed
by the problems inherent in words.

In the field of Vocal Classical Music, to represent its most
popular & distinct form i.e. Khyal, the development of the Raga
around Bandishes or word compositions, is usually done through
alaps & bolalaps; bolbants & boltaans; sargams & finally taans.
These materials give the musical structure of a particular Raga
around the Bandish.

aspects & moods of the same raga are brought out by these various
musical materials.

Comparison of Sargam with Alap & Taan

In the structure of sargam syllables, such as sa, re, ga, ma, pa,
dha, ni—there are four vowels sounds — aa /e/ a/i incorporated in
them. After a sargam syllable is sung, one of these vowels sounds
or formations persist, i.e. once the sargam is uttered what remains
is the vowel in that particular syllable. It therefore takes the
semblance of alap, since alap is also sung through vowels. In a
sense that, sargam syllables include in their structure the effect
inherent in alaps or sounds carried through vowels. By definition,
taan is distinguished from alap by its faster pace. Sargam, however,
can use both the slow pace of alaps & the fast pace of taans.
Thus, sargam can include in itself not just the vowel structure of
alap or taan but also fast movement of taan.

About Mandra Sadhana :

Mandra Sadhana is the traditional method of voice culture in India
which has been followed with equal faith both in the Hindustani
& Kamatic system of Indian Music. It is true that the psychological
method of voice-culture which has been developed in Europe since
the 19" century has no parallel in the Indian culture & secondly
because the requirements of the Indian musical system are fully
satisfied by our traditional method & a method conceived with a
view to fulfilling the requirements of Western music cannot be
efficacious for us. The Indian ideal of musical practice is to forget
the body & to try to transcend the physiological method of voice
culture which demands a consciousness of the movements of
muscles & like does not conform to the Indian conception of Nada
Sadhana which is nothing short of yoga.

Regarding the efficiency of Mandra Sadhana, there is a
personal experience of Pt. Omkarnath Thakur:-

“Until the age of adolescence my voice was very sweet &
perfectly musical with a full range three registers. But at the age
of adolescence my voice broke & became so unmusical &
unpleasant that I had to practically abandon singing & take to
the practice of Mrdanga & Esraj. At the same time I started
Mandra -Sadhana according to the direction given by my
Gurudev, Pandit Vishnu Digambar Paluskar. I used to sit near
the door of his bed room very early in the morning for this practice
& he used to give necessary instructions occasionally. If there is
anything in my voice, it is the result of that Sadhana & I have

A very brief outline of the method of mandra sadhana will
suffice here. This practice should be undertaken preferably early
in the morning before sunrise, because that period is the most
suitable for singing the Mandra Sthana (4% Yi). The first step
of it is to fix one’s Shadja according to the range of one’s voice.
The Shadja should be fixed at a pitch from where one can easily
descend by at least five notes in the Mandra & similarly ascend
by at least five notes in the Taar. One should be very careful in
fixing this pitch-point. The teacher should take special care in
this respect & should never insist on the student’s practice from
the same point which is suitable for the teacher’s own voice. The
fixation of the Shadja at a pitch higher or lower than that suitable
for one’s voice leads to disaster.

Having fixed the shadja at the right pitch-point, one should
try to descend to the lowest possible point in the mandra-sthana.
It should remembered that the lowest possible point must satisfy
the standard of musicality i.e. one’s voice must not lose the quality
of anuranana (3%qX0r) at the lowest point. Having descended to
this point one should repeat the same note with the longest possible
breath for at least fifteen minutes (preferably half an hour). This
repetitive singing of the lowest note may be carried in akara
(3f@R), or aakara (3T@R), or ikara (FMR) or ukara (SHR) or okara
(aN). Pronunciation of each of these vowels along with
prolonged notes in singing implies distinct operation of the vocal
organs & brings about distinct physiological phenomena in the
abdomen, stomach, respiratory organs etc. Those who have special
deficiency in respect of volume of voice should undertake practice
in okara as it tends to increase the volume & promotes angularity
of the voice.

It is to be remembered that mandra sadhana should always be
done with the accompaniment of tanpura. The rich overtones of
tanpura are of great value in bringing about concentration &
